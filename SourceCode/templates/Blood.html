What all you need to know

Blood types are determined by the presence or absence of certain antigens – substances that can trigger an immune response if they are foreign to the body. Since some antigens can trigger a patient's immune system to attack the transfused blood, safe blood transfusions depend on careful blood typing and cross-matching. There are four major blood groups determined by the presence or absence of two antigens, A and B, on the surface of red blood cells. In addition to the A and B antigens, there is a protein called the Rh factor, which can be either present (+) or absent (–), creating the 8 most common blood types (A+, A-, B+, B-, O+, O-, AB+, AB-).

Types of Blood Donors and Receivers

Universal Red Cell Donors: Type O negative (O-) blood can be transfused to anyone with any blood type, making it the universal red cell donor blood. Universal Plasma Donors: Type AB blood is considered the universal plasma donor, as plasma from AB-type donors can be transfused to recipients with A, B, AB, or O blood types.p>

Blood Type Compatibility

    Blood Type Compatibility: Blood type compatibility is crucial for blood transfusions and organ transplants.
ABO Blood Group System: The ABO blood group system categorizes blood into four main types: A, B, AB, and O, based on the presence or absence of specific antigens on the surface of red blood cells. Rh Blood Type System: The Rh blood type system is another important factor in blood type compatibility, with individuals being either Rh-positive or Rh-negative. Compatibility Rules: The rules for blood type compatibility in transfusions are as follows: Universal Donor and Recipient: O-negative: Considered the universal donor, as it can be given to anyone AB-positive: Considered the universal recipient, as it can receive from all other blood types

  • Type A: Can receive from A and O blood types
  • Type B: Can receive from B and O blood types
  • Type O: Can receive from O blood type
  • Type AB: Can receive from AB, A, B, and O blood types.

Eligibility for Donation

Details about who is eligible to donate blood, such as age, weight, and health requirements.

    Age: 18-65 years old
    Weight: At least 50 kg
    Health: In good health at the time of donation, with no cold, flu, sore throat, cold sore, stomach bug, or other infections
    Medical History: No history of: Heart disease Infections such as Hepatitis B, C, Tuberculosis, Leprosy, or HIV Epilepsy, asthma on steroids, bleeding disorders, Thalassemia, Sickle Cell Anemia, or Polycythemia Vera Genital ulcers/discharge, multiple sexual partners, or drug addiction Recent tattoos or body piercings
    Pregnancy and Childbirth: Women must have stopped lactation and be at least 1 year postpartum
    Blood Pressure: Normal systolic and diastolic blood pressures
    Haemoglobin: At least 12.5 grams

Importance of Blood Donations

Blood donations are essential for Saving lives by providing a constant supply of blood for transfusions. Helping patients with diverse medical needs, including injuries, cancer, and surgical procedures. Improving heart health and reducing the risk of heart attacks. Fostering a sense of community and providing financial compensation. Reducing iron levels and potentially lowering the risk of certain cancers.
